# Product Specification: Personas & Journeys

This document defines *who* uses the Studio OS and *how* they use it. This is Level 3 — the specific implementation for creative studios.

## The Paradigm Shift

Traditional studio software hardcodes "sessions" and "shoots" into the system. If the studio wants to add videography or change their workflow, the system breaks.

The Studio OS places the universal production lifecycle at the center. Studios configure their own services, workflows, and vocabulary. The OS does not dictate *how* a studio operates — it provides the universal engine and lets each studio configure their own identity.

---

## Personas

### The Organization (The Studio)
The automated brain. It is the Production Engine configured for a specific creative business. When a service is purchased, the Organization automatically reserves resources, generates financial transactions, routes tasks to staff, and expresses itself to the public through the Visual Engine.

### The Configurator (Studio Owner / Manager)
The human who programs the Organization. They define services, design workflows, set pricing rules, and use the Ubiquitous Visual Engine to design every client-facing touchpoint. Their goal: build a business engine that delivers a premium client experience while minimizing their own operational overhead.

### The Operator (Studio Staff)
The human assigned to execute work. They log in to see tasks routed to them by the Organization. They check out resources, log their time, produce assets, and advance workflow stages.

### The Recipient (The Client)
The external person who creates intent and receives deliverables. They interact with the studio through surfaces designed by the Configurator using the Visual Engine — storefronts, booking pages, galleries, invoices.

## The Core Journey

The universal production lifecycle, configured for a creative studio:

### 1. Intent
A client wants something. They fill out an inquiry form on the studio's storefront, send a DM, or walk in. The system captures this as an Intent — the raw desire for value.

### 2. Planning
The Configurator refines the Intent into a specific scope. They select a service template, adjust the pricing, and generate a proposal. The proposal is designed using the Visual Engine — it is not a static PDF but a branded, interactive page.

### 3. Commitment
The client accepts. An Agreement is created. The system automatically generates the required Financial Transactions (deposit invoice) and processes payment. The Agreement locks the terms.

### 4. Orchestration
The system spawns the Workflow defined by the service template. It reserves the required resources (cameras, studio space), assigns staff to tasks, and blocks the calendar. Conflicts are automatically flagged.

### 5. Production
Staff execute the workflow stages. For a photography studio: Capture → Cull → Edit. For a music studio: Record → Mix → Master. Each stage produces assets and advances the workflow.

### 6. Evaluation
Quality gate. Internal review, manager sign-off, or client approval — depending on how the Configurator set up the workflow. Prevents subpar deliverables from reaching the client.

### 7. Delivery
The Configurator uses the Visual Engine to design a branded delivery experience — a client gallery with a print store, a download portal, or a streaming page. The client receives their deliverables.

### 8. Commerce
The client can purchase additional value — prints, albums, licensing. These generate new Financial Transactions in the same ledger. Passive revenue with zero additional production cost.

### 9. Archive & Learning
The completed cycle is archived with its full relational graph. The system surfaces insights: average turnaround time, profit margins per service, resource utilization rates. This feeds improvements into future cycles.
